We believe in purpose-driven work that prioritizes student growth, ethical AI, and measurable impact. Our team combines deep expertise in education, technology, and design to create solutions that truly matter.

Our Guiding Principles

Our work is grounded in enduring principles that form foundation of integrity, discipline, and collective responsibility. These values guide every decision we make and every product we build.

Surah An-Najm (53:39)

“And that there is not for man except that [good] for which he strives.”

How this shapes our culture and work: effort is non-transferable, so ownership and disciplined execution are baseline expectations across teams.

Surah Al-Hujurat (49:11)

“O believers! Do not let some ˹men˺ ridicule others... How evil it is to act rebelliously after having faith!”

How this shapes our culture and work: respect is operational, not decorative; we challenge ideas without diminishing people, roles, or institutions.

Surah As-Saff (61:2)

“Why do you say what you do not do?”

How this shapes our culture and work: commitments must be traceable to delivery; we do not make claims we cannot evidence in product behavior.

Surah Al-Mu'minun (23:8)

“Those who are true to their trusts and covenants.”

How this shapes our culture and work: learner trust, school commitments, and data obligations are treated as contracts we honor without exception.

Surah Ash-Shuraa (42:19)

“Allah is Ever Kind to His servants... And He is All-Powerful”

How this shapes our culture and work: we pair rigor with care, designing systems that are effective, humane, and proportionate for students and teachers.

Our Interpretation

These principles inform our code of conduct, product integrity, and collaborative practices — emphasizing accountability, humility, and measurable contribution to education.

How We See Work

Work at tinker is not defined by output. It is defined by what that output builds toward.

Three pillars run through every role, every roadmap, every decision made inside this organisation. They are not aspirational. They are operational — the lens through which we review work, resolve conflict, and determine what gets built next.

Institutional Craft

We build for the long term. Not the demo, not the pitch — the third year of a school's deployment, when the system is being used by teachers who never attended a training session, on devices that are two generations old, in a context no product manager visited. That is the environment we design for.

Durable defaults over one-off wins. Documentation that future members of this team will actually need. Incentives aligned to learning outcomes — because a metric that doesn't connect to how a child learns is not a metric worth optimising for.

Truthful Measurement

What we claim, we can show. That is not a value — it is a working condition.

Evidence, audit trails, and honest disclosure of model limitations and data boundaries come before any release. We expose uncertainty. We surface failure modes. We pair qualitative signal from classrooms with quantitative checks — because neither alone is sufficient and both together are still incomplete without the humility to say so.

We invest in evaluation before expansion. Especially when minors are in the loop. The bar does not move because a deadline is close.

Shared Guardrails

Safety and privacy are not assigned to one team. They are constraints that every function designs against from the beginning — product, engineering, education, operations.

We bring policy, design, and engineering into the same room early. We respond to issues proportionately, transparently, and without blame displacement. We keep escalation paths legible — for internal teams, for schools, and for families. When something surfaces, the path forward is already known.

Ethics and Values

These are the non-negotiables. Not statements of aspiration — criteria for how we hire, how we perform, and what it feels like to work and partner with tinker.

Integrity over narrative.

We say no when speed would break trust. We document decisions that affect learners and data — not for compliance, but because decisions with consequences deserve a record. We do not construct a version of the truth that is easier to communicate. We communicate the truth.

Humility in expertise.

Teachers and families are co-authors of product truth. The person in the classroom every day knows something we do not, and that knowledge is not optional input — it is the ground our work stands on. We invite challenge. Title does not end debate. Seniority does not make a position correct.

Speed with consent.

We move fast where risk is bounded. We slow down where minors or sensitive data are involved — and that slowdown is not reluctance, it is the appropriate response to the responsibility we carry. We prefer explicit opt-in for any new surface that changes classroom dynamics. The default is caution, not momentum.

Transparency with partners.

Plain language on capabilities, costs, and roadmaps — to every school leader, every institutional partner, every decision-maker who carries liability for what we build together. No black-box promises. No commitments we cannot trace back to something real.

Care for learners.

Accessibility, psychological safety, and proportionate data use are product requirements. Not considerations — requirements. We measure success in sustained engagement and genuine growth. Not retention engineered through habit loops. Not sessions extended past the point of learning. Growth. The kind that shows up in a student's ability to think, not in a dashboard.

How Our Work Impacts Students' Lives

Every role here touches what a child actually experiences.

Not abstractly. Concretely — how feedback arrives after a wrong answer, how a mistake is framed inside the system, how agency is practised inside a learning path, how a parent understands what their child did today and what it means for tomorrow. These are not UX decisions. They are consequential decisions that happen to be expressed through interface and system behaviour.

We hire people who feel the weight of that. Who understand that the system they are building is not neutral — that every design choice carries a position on what learning should feel like, what a child's relationship with difficulty should be, and what adults owe children when they are trusted with their development.

That weight does not produce paralysis. It produces rigour.

If your work improves teacher judgment — you are returning attention to the student. If it clarifies a learning path — you are reducing the friction between a child and something they are trying to understand. If it reduces administrative drag — you are giving back time that was never meant to be spent on a spreadsheet. All of it flows to the child.

That is the impact bar we use in prioritisation. Not sessions. Not activations. Not growth-week numbers. The question is simple: does this make the learning better, the teacher more capable, or the institution more able to serve the student in front of it?

If the answer is yes — it moves forward. If the answer is unclear — we find out before we ship.
